The Co-Living between Reality and Virtuality as a Daily Routine

Mixed Reality (MR) is quickly becoming part of everyday life — from AI-powered XR wearables to seamless transitions between physical and virtual environments. This workshop brings together researchers, designers, and practitioners to explore inclusive onboarding and interaction techniques for a future where moving across the reality–virtuality continuum becomes routine.

Overview

We are approaching a moment where MR is poised to become a new standard of reality: users may switch between full VR, passthrough MR, and the physical world as part of day-to-day routines. While many CHI attendees have deep experience with immersive systems, broad adoption demands onboarding strategies that work for people with diverse backgrounds — including generational differences, cultural contexts, disabilities, and neurodivergence.

Key questions

  • Seamless transitions: How can moving between reality and virtuality become intuitive and comfortable?
  • Inclusive onboarding: What interaction patterns support first-time adopters and underrepresented groups?
  • Everyday MR: How do we design for routines like commuting, healthcare, education, or home activities?

What to expect

  • Short framing insights and guided discussion
  • Lightning presentations of accepted contributions
  • Poster/demo-style prototype sharing
  • Breakout group co-design and synthesis of principles

Call for participation

This workshop explores how MR is becoming embedded in everyday routines and asks how we can design seamless, inclusive, and meaningful transitions between the physical and the virtual.

Topics of interest

  • Seamless transitions: metaphors, interface concepts, and technical solutions across VR/MR/physical contexts
  • Onboarding & accessibility: inclusive interaction patterns across culture, disabilities, and differing tech experience
  • Embodied everyday interaction: cooking, commuting, healthcare, education, work
  • Speculative & critical perspectives: ethics, opting in/out, continuous recording and privacy
  • Prototypes & demonstrations: systems, mock-ups, artworks, and future scenarios

Submission types

  • Short papers: up to 6 pages
  • Position papers: 2–4 pages
  • Poster/demo abstracts: 1 page

Workshop activities

Planned 180 minutes total (two 90-minute sessions). Times may be adapted to the final CHI schedule.

Segment Duration Description
1. Opening & scene setting 15 min Framing XR as everyday tech; guiding questions on seamless transitions and inclusiveness.
2. Paper presentations 50 min Accepted papers/position papers presented (5–7 minutes each) to seed discussion.
3. Debate & critical reflections 25 min Moderated discussion on ethics, privacy, and whether disengagement should remain an option.
— Break — 15 min Reset and informal discussion.
4. Prototype demos & posters 20 min Interactive sharing of demos/posters (e.g., carrying virtual objects across transitions, scalable text overlays).
5. Breakout groups & co-design 40 min Small-group sketching/prototyping focused on inclusiveness, everyday usability, and ethics.
6. Sharing & synthesis 20 min Groups present; extraction of design principles and open research challenges.
7. Wrap-up & next steps 10 min Outline publication plans, collaboration channels, and post-workshop archival.

Accessibility

We are committed to making the workshop accessible and inclusive. Plans include ensuring wheelchair-accessible space, flexible seating, accessible materials in advance (embedded-text PDFs), multimodal instructions (spoken and written), and inclusive facilitation that supports varied participation formats.

At the venue

  • Wheelchair accessible room and flexible seating arrangements
  • Activities designed to avoid excessive standing or movement
  • Facilitation to ensure all voices are included

Materials & sensory considerations

  • Accessible PDFs with embedded text
  • Posters accompanied by short text summaries; demos include captions/alt-text
  • Instructions given both verbally and in writing
